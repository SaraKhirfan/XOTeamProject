Blood & Circulatory System

Gorem ipsum dolor sit amet, consectetur adipiscing elit.

In space, the circulatory system undergoes major adaptations due to microgravity (NASA Cardiovascular Health in Microgravity). Body fluids shift from the legs toward the head and chest (Station Science 101: Cardiovascular Research), leading to reduced overall blood volume (NASA Cardiovascular Health), facial puffiness, and “bird legs.” Over time, the heart remodels—losing muscle mass and changing shape—while arteries may stiffen and vascular regulation weakens (Station Science 101; PMC Review on Cardiovascular System in Space). These changes reduce the body’s ability to maintain blood pressure, often causing dizziness or fainting when astronauts return to Earth (NASA Cardiovascular Health). NASA studies like Vascular Echo, Fluid Shifts, and engineered heart tissue experiments on the ISS help track these effects and develop countermeasures to protect cardiovascular health during long-duration missions (Station Science 101).

Microbiome (Gut & Skin)

Astronaut microbiome studies via NASA’s Open Science Data Repository show that spaceflight and long-duration confinement led to shifts in gut microbial communities, including reductions in beneficial species that degrade resistant starch and suppress inflammation (e.g. Faecalibacterium prausnitzii, Ruminococcus bromii). NASA Salivary (oral / upper gut) microbiome alterations are also tracked in experiments like OSD-280, monitoring changes before, during, and after flight. NASA Science On the environmental side, microbial surveys of spacecraft surfaces (e.g. OSD-65) help map how the built environment’s microbiome interacts with and potentially influences crew skin or habitat microbiomes. Future Opportunities refer to the possibilities and prospects that may emerge over time as a result of technological advancements, economic shifts, and social transformations.
