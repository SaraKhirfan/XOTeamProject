HUMAN

Studying humans in space highlights many research gaps that remain critical for future exploration. Despite decades of missions, we still lack full understanding of how long-term exposure to microgravity and radiation affects the human body, especially the immune system, cardiovascular health, and bone density. These gaps pose risks for astronauts on missions to Mars or extended stays on the Moon. Identifying such unknowns allows researchers and mission planners to develop countermeasures, create safer environments, and ensure sustainable human presence beyond Earth.

PLANT

Plants are essential for long-term human space missions, providing food, oxygen, and psychological benefits. However, significant research gaps exist in understanding how microgravity, radiation, and limited resources affect plant growth, reproduction, and nutrient content. Scientists are still exploring optimal conditions for cultivating plants on the Moon or Mars, including soil substitutes, water recycling, and light cycles. Addressing these gaps is crucial for developing sustainable life-support systems, ensuring reliable food production, and supporting crew health during extended space exploration missions.

MICROBES

Microorganisms play a vital role in both human health and spacecraft safety, yet many gaps remain in understanding their behavior in space. In microgravity, microbes can change how they grow, spread, and resist antibiotics, which may increase risks of infection for astronauts. They also pose challenges for planetary protection, as certain microbes might survive harsh space environments and contaminate other worlds. By identifying these gaps, researchers can design better monitoring systems, develop stronger countermeasures, and ensure both astronaut health and the integrity of future missions to the Moon and Mars

CELLS

Cells are the fundamental units of life, and understanding how they respond to space conditions is crucial. Major gaps remain in knowing how microgravity and space radiation affect cell growth, division, signaling, and genetic stability. These changes can influence the health of tissues, organs, and whole organisms, including humans, plants, and microbes. By studying these cellular responses, scientists can develop strategies to mitigate harmful effects, improve regenerative medicine approaches, and ensure safer long-term missions to the Moon, Mars, and beyond.

RADIATION

Radiation in space is one of the most significant risks to human health and biological systems. Research gaps remain in understanding how cosmic rays and solar particle events affect DNA, cells, and organs over long-duration missions. High-energy radiation can increase the risk of cancer, cardiovascular issues, and immune system dysfunction. Studying these effects is crucial for developing effective shielding, protective measures, and medical countermeasures to ensure the safety and wellbeing of astronauts during missions to the Moon, Mars, and deep space exploration.
